Chamber of Echoes

Kantian Deontology

Maria's Essays • 2025-08-14

When Kant claims "lying is wrong," the metaethical question arises: what type of fact is this? Kant insists moral truths are synthetic a priori, known through pure reason yet substantive about the world. Consider the truthmaker puzzle: what makes "never lie" true? Unlike empirical facts grounded in physical states, Kant locates moral facts in practical reason's structure. But if reason itself serves as truthmaker, we face immediate problems. Take the prohibition on false promises: does this fact exist because reason's consistency requirements make deceptive promising impossible to universalize, or does reason discover pre-existing moral facts? The grounding problem emerges when examining autonomous will: Kant claims we legislate moral law through freedom, yet these self-given laws supposedly bind us categorically. Consider a specific case: if I autonomously will the maxim "help others in distress," what transforms my willing into an objective moral fact that binds all rational agents? The formula of universal law appears to test rather than generate moral facts. When evaluating "steal when convenient," the universalizability test reveals contradiction, but this seems to presuppose rather than establish stealing's wrongness. If moral facts derive from pure practical reason, we must explain reason's authority: why should consistency requirements generate moral rather than merely logical constraints?

Kant's synthetic a priori moral truths face severe metaethical challenges. Take "respect humanity as an end-in-itself": this supposedly combines a priori necessity with substantive content, but how does analyzing reason alone yield facts about how to treat humans? Mathematical analogies prove unhelpful: while "7+5=12" might emerge from analyzing number concepts, "respect rational nature" seems to require more than conceptual unpacking. Consider the formalism objection through concrete examples. The maxim "whistle Dixie at 3 PM on Wednesdays" passes universalizability without contradiction, yet generates no genuine moral fact. Conversely, "lie to save innocent lives" might fail universalizability despite strong moral grounds. What does this reveal about moral facts' nature? Even sophisticated reformulations cannot escape empty formalism. O'Neill's procedural reading emphasizes maxims others could adopt, but consider "always wear blue shirts": everyone could adopt this without contradiction, yet no moral fact emerges. The formula of humanity seems more substantive: "treat humanity never merely as means," but this smuggles in the substantive premise that rational nature has special value. Without this hidden axiom, pure reason cannot generate the specific content that humanity deserves respect rather than, say, that complexity or beauty deserves promotion.

Intuitionist objections reveal deep problems with Kantian moral epistemology. When witnessing someone torture an animal for amusement, we don't derive wrongness through universalizability procedures but perceive it directly. Consider the phenomenology: seeing a child being beaten, moral wrongness presents itself immediately, not as the conclusion of a practical syllogism but as a given feature of the situation. Take promise-breaking: the obligation seems to arise from the promise itself, grasped through moral perception, rather than from testing maxims against rational procedures. Kantian epistemology cannot explain this immediacy. If someone pushes another off a subway platform, we don't think "could this be universalized?" but directly apprehend the act's moral status. The universalizability test often conflicts with clear intuitions: "kill one healthy patient to save five dying ones" might pass formal universalizability ( everyone could will a world where utility is maximized ) yet violates our direct perception of murder's wrongness. Moral disagreement poses another challenge: if morality is accessible through pure reason that all humans share, why do thoughtful people reach opposite conclusions about abortion, euthanasia, or wealth distribution? The intuitionist explanation proves simpler: we possess fallible moral perception that sometimes differs, rather than pure reason mysteriously yielding contradictory results. When someone judges "gratuitous suffering is bad," they report perceiving badness in suffering itself, not discovering what maxims can be universalized.

The fact-value gap presents fundamental challenges to Kantian metaethics. Even granting that reason demands consistency, how does this generate moral facts about our duties to others? Consider a specific case: reason might require that I not will contradictory maxims, but why does this create facts about what I owe strangers? Kant claims practical reason is inherently normative, but this seems to conflate two senses of "practical": reason can be practical in motivating action without generating moral facts. Take the universalizability requirement: even if I cannot consistently will everyone to lie, this shows lying is imprudent or irrational, not that it violates moral facts. The humanity formula attempts to bridge this gap: humans possess rational nature, therefore deserve respect. But examine this inference: even granting humans are rational, what transforms this descriptive fact into the normative fact that rationality grounds dignity? We could equally note that humans possess sentience, creativity, or mortality without these features generating moral facts. The Humean challenge remains unmet: Kant derives ought from is by stipulating that rational nature has value, but this stipulation either assumes moral facts ( begging the question ) or creates them arbitrarily ( undermining objectivity ) . Consider beneficence: even if helping others could be universally willed, this shows compatibility with reason, not that reason generates a fact making beneficence obligatory.

Kantian moral facts face a fundamental truthmaker dilemma. If moral facts are constituted by what rational agents can consistently will as universal law, their ontological status remains mysterious. Consider a world without rational beings: would "torture is wrong" lack a truthmaker and therefore be neither true nor false? This seems to make moral facts unacceptably mind-dependent. Yet if moral facts exist independently of rational willing, then Kant's constructivism collapses into intuitionist realism. Take a specific moral fact: "promises should be kept. " On constructivist readings, this fact exists because rational agents cannot consistently will promise-breaking. But in possible worlds lacking rational agents, promises cannot exist, suggesting the moral fact's truthmaker depends on contingent features of worlds containing promisers. Contemporary Kantian constructivists like Korsgaard argue we create moral facts through reflective endorsement: when I reflectively endorse helping others, this creates a fact about my obligations. But consider what makes some endorsements correct: if nothing, then moral facts are arbitrary constructions; if something, then moral facts pre-exist construction. The noumenal freedom solution fares no better: even if we're free in the noumenal realm, why should exercises of this freedom generate moral rather than aesthetic or prudential facts? Each attempt to ground moral facts in reason either presupposes their existence or generates them arbitrarily. Kantian metaethics ultimately reveals the impossibility of deriving substantive moral facts from pure practical reason: without smuggling in intuitions about value, dignity, or obligation, formal procedures yield only empty consistency requirements, not the rich moral facts that govern actual ethical life.
