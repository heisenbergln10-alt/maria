Chamber of Echoes

Error theory

Maria's Essays • 2025-08-14

When someone asserts "torturing innocents is wrong," error theory agrees this statement purports to describe an objective moral fact but insists no such fact exists, making the statement false like "this substance contains phlogiston" or "the ether carries light waves. " Error theorists accept that moral discourse aims at truth but deny the existence of moral truthmakers. If someone claims "genocide is evil," they attempt to attribute an objective property to genocide, but since no such property exists, the statement is systematically false. Mackie's argument from queerness suggests moral facts would need intrinsically motivating and categorically binding properties unlike anything else in nature. Consider a moral fact like "promise-breaking is wrong": this would need to be a fact that necessarily provides reasons for action regardless of anyone's desires, a metaphysically peculiar entity. But this argument might beg the question against moral realism. Perhaps moral properties are sui generis, just as phenomenal redness differs from wavelength properties, or mathematical objects exist platonically. Error theory must explain why moral discourse systematically fails to refer while other domains succeed. When we say "electrons have negative charge" or "water is H2O," we successfully refer to real properties, but error theorists claim "kindness is virtuous" fails to refer to anything. This asymmetry needs justification: what makes moral properties uniquely impossible?

Error theory's semantic claims about moral language face serious challenges. When a judge declares "the defendant acted unjustly," error theory claims she attempts to describe an objective moral fact but necessarily fails, like a phrenologist claiming "this skull shape indicates criminality. " The judge thinks she's attributing a real property of injustice, but she's actually projecting subjective attitudes onto a morally neutral world. Consider everyday moral claims: when someone says "insider trading is dishonest," they believe they're identifying an objective feature of the practice, not merely expressing disapproval. Error theorists insist moral concepts build in objective purport, making all moral claims false when this purport fails. But do moral concepts really require such metaphysical robustness? Perhaps "wrong" means something more modest like "prohibited by ideal social rules" rather than "possessing intrinsic not-to-be-doneness. " The companions in guilt argument challenges error theory's selective skepticism: colors face the problem of appearing intrinsic to objects while being partially mind-dependent, numbers exist abstractly without causal powers, and modal claims about possibility and necessity invoke metaphysically puzzling properties. If we accept these despite ontological queerness, why reject moral properties? Error theory faces a dilemma: either moral concepts are so metaphysically demanding that nothing could possibly satisfy them ( making error theory trivially true but uninteresting ) or they're modest enough that naturalistic properties might satisfy them ( undermining the view's motivation ) .

Intuitionist objections highlight how error theory contradicts moral phenomenology. When witnessing someone deliberately burning a child with cigarettes, we don't infer wrongness through complex reasoning but perceive it directly alongside the physical violence. The moral wrongness presents itself as immediately as the screaming or the smell of burning flesh. Consider other examples: seeing someone push an elderly person down stairs, watching a mob attack an innocent victim, or observing someone poisoning a neighbor's pet for amusement. In each case, wrongness seems phenomenologically given, not constructed or projected. Error theorists deploy debunking arguments: evolutionary pressures shaped our moral beliefs for cooperation and survival, not truth-tracking. But this proves too much. Evolution also shaped our perceptual beliefs about predators, food, and mates, yet we don't conclude all perceptual beliefs are false. Why should moral perception be uniquely unreliable? Examine moral certainty: we're more confident that torturing babies for fun is wrong than in any philosophical argument for error theory. The premises supporting error theory ( about metaphysical queerness or evolutionary debunking ) seem less certain than basic moral truths. This suggests error theory might be self-defeating: if we can't trust our clearest moral intuitions, why trust the subtler philosophical intuitions driving error theory? Denying moral perception requires explaining away massive phenomenological evidence that moral properties present themselves directly in experience.

Error theory renders moral practice and deliberation incoherent. If all moral claims are false, moral reasoning becomes as pointless as debating unicorn breeding techniques or perpetual motion machine designs. When someone deliberates whether to reveal a friend's embarrassing secret, error theory suggests they're reasoning about non-existent properties, like consulting astrology charts for investment advice. Consider practical deliberation: a doctor deciding whether to respect a patient's autonomy or paternalistically override their wishes supposedly weighs non-existent moral properties. The schizophrenia problem emerges starkly: error theorists must simultaneously believe "racial discrimination is wrong" is false while continuing to oppose discrimination. This creates pragmatic contradictions. When teaching children not to bully, error theorist parents must think "bullying is cruel" is false while acting as if it's true. Fictionalism attempts rescue by suggesting we should pretend moral facts exist, like acting within the fiction of a play. But this faces the privileging problem: if "respect others" and "exploit others" are equally false, what non-moral reason justifies choosing one fiction over another? The very act of advocating error theory presupposes we ought to believe truth over falsehood, but this normative ought would itself be false on error theory. Moral practice requires participants to believe their claims can be true, making error theory pragmatically self-refuting.

Sophisticated error theory cannot preserve morality's core features while denying moral facts. Joyce's fictionalism suggests maintaining moral discourse as useful fiction, like keeping Santa Claus stories for children's benefit. But what makes the fiction of human rights more useful than the fiction of racial hierarchy without smuggling in moral facts about what we should value? Consider choosing between incompatible moral fictions: "maximize happiness" versus "maximize authenticity" versus "maximize honor. " If all are equally false, selecting among them requires moral grounds that error theory denies. Conservationist error theorists want to preserve existing moral practices while denying their truth, but this seems arbitrary. Why preserve condemnation of cruelty rather than past practices of honor killing or dueling? Revolutionary error theory more consistently advocates abandoning moral discourse entirely, but consider the costs: could society function without concepts of justice, rights, or obligations? Criminal law would reduce to mere power enforcement, promises would lack binding force, and ethical constraints on research would disappear. The companions in innocence strategy reveals error theory's implausible selectivity: we face metaphysical puzzles about epistemic norms ( why ought we believe truth? ) , colors ( are they mind-independent? ) , mathematics ( how do abstract objects exist? ) , and modality ( what grounds possibility? ) . Yet we remain realists about these domains despite ontological challenges. Error theory commits the fundamental mistake of demanding that moral facts fit a preconceived naturalistic ontology, then declaring them non-existent when they don't conform. By denying moral truth, error theory creates an unstable position that undermines the very practices it often seeks to preserve, ultimately offering an incoherent metaethics that neither explains moral experience nor provides liveable guidance.
