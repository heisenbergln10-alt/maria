Chamber of Echoes

Utilitarianism

Maria's Essays • 2025-08-14

When sophisticated utilitarian theories claim "torture is wrong," they face immediate metaethical questions about what type of fact this represents. Consider act utilitarianism claiming torture's wrongness: is this fact identical to neurological pain states, reduced to preference frustration, or supervening on welfare diminishment? Even complex variants incorporating side-constraints must explain their foundations. Take rights-respecting utilitarianism asserting "respect human dignity while maximizing utility. " What grounds the dignity constraint: does respecting dignity produce more utility ( making it derivative ) , or does dignity possess independent moral status ( abandoning pure utilitarianism ) ? Examine a specific case: if torturing one terrorist prevents a nuclear attack, the utilitarian adding "respect rights" as a constraint must explain what makes rights morally significant. The truthmaker problem emerges starkly: if moral facts are made true by consequences, what serves as truthmaker for "maximize good consequences"? The principle cannot be made true by its own consequences without vicious circularity. Consider preference utilitarianism claiming "satisfy preferences": what makes preference satisfaction morally significant rather than, say, maximizing the number of prime numbers? Even sophisticated theories adding fairness constraints face infinite regress: if "distribute utility fairly" is justified by producing more utility, fairness becomes instrumental; if justified independently, the theory admits non-utilitarian moral facts.

Utilitarian metaethics fundamentally transforms moral properties' metaphysical status. Consider justice: intuitionists might regard it as a sui generis property accessible through rational intuition, while utilitarians must reduce it to utility patterns. When declaring "this income distribution is unjust," the intuitionist perceives irreducible unfairness, but the utilitarian translates this into "this distribution produces suboptimal utility. " Examine courage: for virtue ethicists, courage represents an irreducible excellence of character, but utilitarians redefine it as utility-maximizing risk-taking. A soldier jumping on a grenade exhibits courage for the virtue theorist regardless of consequences; for utilitarians, it's only courageous if it maximizes expected utility. Consider rights: when sophisticated utilitarians add "respect property rights," they must either reduce property rights to utility-maximizing conventions ( eliminating their deontic force ) or accept irreducible moral constraints ( abandoning utilitarian reduction ) . Take promise-keeping: the intuitionist sees a sui generis obligation created by the speech act, while utilitarians reduce it to expectation management and social coordination benefits. Even two-level utilitarianism distinguishing intuitive from critical thinking transforms virtues into useful heuristics rather than genuine moral properties. The semantic shift proves revealing: "honesty" no longer denotes a moral excellence but merely a generally utility-promoting behavioral pattern.

The intuitionist epistemological challenge strikes at utilitarian knowledge claims. If moral facts concern consequences, how do we access these sprawling causal chains? Consider choosing whether to donate to malaria prevention versus education: the utilitarian requires impossible calculations about centuries of ripple effects, while the intuitionist might directly perceive obligations to help those in dire need. Take a specific judgment: "insider trading is wrong. " The intuitionist grasps this through rational intuition about fairness and trust, while the utilitarian must calculate market efficiency, wealth distribution, and institutional effects across indefinite time horizons. Address the idealization: even if ideal observers could trace all consequences, why should their calculations constitute moral truth rather than sophisticated prediction? We know with greater certainty that torturing innocents for sadistic pleasure is wrong than we could know any utilitarian calculation about its effects. Consider sophisticated versions adding dignity or autonomy: how do we know these constraints? If through consequences, we face circularity ( dignity matters because respecting it has good consequences, but what makes consequences matter? ) . If through intuition, utilitarian epistemology is abandoned. The certainty asymmetry proves damning: our confidence that genocide is wrong exceeds any possible confidence in utilitarian calculations supposedly grounding this judgment.

Utilitarian revisionism reveals deep metaethical instability. Each epicycle added to handle counterexamples suggests moral reality resists utilitarian capture. Consider rule utilitarianism adopting "never torture": if this rule is correct because following it maximizes utility, then exceptions should exist when torture would produce better consequences; if no exceptions exist, the rule's validity transcends utilitarian justification. Examine two-level theories: when critical thinking overrides intuitive rules, what validates this hierarchy? If utility determines when to override intuitions, we need to know consequences before knowing whether to calculate consequences, generating paradox. Take government house utilitarianism claiming the true theory should be hidden: if moral truth includes "believing the truth has bad consequences," this creates self-reference problems about what moral facts actually are. Consider adding agent-relative permissions: "you may save your child over two strangers. " If this permission exists because people with such permissions create more utility overall, it's not genuinely agent-relative; if it's irreducibly agent-relative, non-utilitarian moral facts are admitted. Motive restrictions show similar problems: if motives matter only instrumentally, the theory remains utilitarian but can't capture moral phenomenology; if motives matter intrinsically, the theory abandons utilitarian reduction. These accommodations reveal that sophisticated utilitarianism becomes either unstable ( constantly adding ad hoc exceptions ) or self-defeating ( admitting irreducible moral facts ) .

Even granting utilitarian naturalism, the normative authority problem remains unsolved. If moral facts reduce to or supervene on facts about well-being, what gives these particular natural facts their prescriptive force? Consider Mill's proof: even knowing that happiness is universally desired, one can coherently ask "but ought I promote it?" suggesting an unbridged gap between natural and moral facts. Take a specific reduction: "wrongness is the property of diminishing aggregate welfare. " This treats a moral property as identical to a natural property, but why should this natural property command allegiance rather than others like "maximizing biodiversity" or "increasing complexity"? Constitutivism offers no escape: if moral facts are constituted by utility facts, what grounds this constitution relation? When sophisticated theories incorporate deontic constraints ( "never violate rights" ) , agent-centered prerogatives ( "you may favor loved ones" ) , or virtue elements ( "act from good motives" ) , trace their truthmakers. Either these elements reduce to consequences ( making them eliminable in principle ) or require independent grounds ( abandoning utilitarian metaethics ) . Consider a theory claiming "maximize utility while respecting autonomy, fairness, and dignity": either these constraints are ultimately grounded in utility ( making them derivative and eliminable when calculations dictate ) or they represent irreducible moral facts ( admitting intuitionist metaethics ) . The sophisticated utilitarian faces a trilemma: preserve utilitarian reduction by eliminating moral facts' distinctive normativity, maintain normativity by covertly embracing intuitionism, or accept eliminativism about genuinely prescriptive moral facts. Even the most complex utilitarian theories cannot escape this metaethical bind, revealing that moral facts' irreducible normativity resists any naturalistic reduction that would preserve both their truth and their action-guiding force.
