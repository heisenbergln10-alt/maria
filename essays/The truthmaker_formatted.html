Chamber of Echoes

The truthmaker

Maria's Essays • 2025-08-14

The truthmaker principle maintains that truth is grounded in being: for every true proposition p, there exists some entity or entities whose existence necessitates p's truth. Armstrong's necessitarian framework requires that truthmaker T necessitates p such that T's existence entails p's truth across all possible worlds, while Lewis objects that such necessitation proves too strong given counterpart theory's treatment of de re modal claims. The Category Problem emerges when considering whether concrete particulars can serve as truthmakers for propositions about abstract objects: if mathematical truths require truthmakers, must we accept either that concrete objects somehow necessitate facts about abstracta or that states of affairs exist as sui generis entities bridging the categorial divide? The Entailment Principle faces serious challenges from contingent predication and temporal propositions, since an object's mere existence cannot entail that it possesses contingent properties at particular times without supplementation by additional temporal or property-instantiation facts. These difficulties suggest that genuine truthmaking requires hyperintensional in-virtue-of relations beyond mere modal correlation, as the existence of Socrates alone cannot make true that Socrates is sitting without invoking either temporally indexed states of affairs or thick particulars that essentially involve property instantiations. The necessitation requirement thus proves both too weak in failing to capture explanatory priority and too strong in demanding impossible entailments from thin particulars to contingent truths, indicating that truthmaker theory must either embrace states of affairs as fundamental truthmakers or abandon strict necessitation for a more nuanced grounding relation.

The Negative Existential Problem reveals how truthmaker theory struggles with truths that seemingly concern absence rather than presence: what entity makes true that unicorns do not exist? Russell's introduction of negative facts as irreducible truthmakers preserves systematic truthmaking but at the cost of accepting fundamentally negative entities that many find ontologically suspect, while the Totality State solution posits a fact that all existing things are thus-and-so, which seems to merely relocate rather than solve the problem. Cheater-Catching strategies that identify positive truthmakers for negative existentials face the challenge of explaining how facts about actual existents necessitate claims about what does not exist without implicitly invoking totality. The Problem of General Truths compounds these difficulties, as universal generalizations appear to require either dubious totality facts or an infinity of particular truthmakers, neither option proving satisfactory. Disjunctive truths present a parallel challenge: must we accept truthmaker gaps for disjunctions whose disjuncts lack individual truthmakers, posit disjunctive states of affairs that violate the principle that truthmakers should be more fundamental than the truths they ground, or embrace a many-many truthmaking relation that abandons unique minimal truthmakers? Fine's essentialist framework offers supervenience without necessitation as a middle path, where truths supervene on reality in virtue of essential connections rather than modal entailment, potentially allowing negative and general truths to be grounded in positive particular facts through essential rather than modal connections. However, this approach requires accepting hyperintensional machinery that some find more problematic than the original difficulties it addresses.

The Molnar Problem concerns propositions about non-existent objects: what makes true that Sherlock Holmes is a detective rather than a doctor? Standard truthmaker theory seems to require that truths about fictional characters have truthmakers, yet no concrete entities appear suitable for this role, and abstract entities like fictional characters themselves seem insufficiently robust to ground truths about their properties. Presentist approaches to truths about the past face analogous difficulties: if only present entities exist, what makes true that Caesar crossed the Rubicon? Lucretian properties that embed historical information in present objects seem ad hoc, ersatz times threaten to collapse into abstractionist theories that abandon genuine truthmaking, and brute past-tensed facts appear to violate the truthmaker principle itself. The Slingshot Argument poses a distinct challenge by purporting to show that if necessary truths have truthmakers, then all necessary truths share the same truthmaker, thereby undermining truthmaker theory's ambition to provide fine-grained explanations of individual truths. While responses involving structured propositions or facts can block the argument's substitutions, they require commitments to hyperintensional entities that sit uneasily with truthmaker theory's supposed ontological austerity. Higher-order quantification over truthmakers generates revenge problems when we ask what makes true that certain truthmaking relations obtain: either we need second-order truthmakers for first-order truthmaking facts, launching a regress, or we must accept brute truths about truthmaking itself, undermining the theory's completeness aspirations.

The Access Problem challenges truthmaker theory's epistemic implications: if knowing that p requires epistemic access to p's truthmaker, then most ordinary knowledge becomes impossible since we rarely have direct acquaintance with the states of affairs or property instantiations that supposedly ground truths. This threatens to impose an implausible intellectualist requirement whereby knowledge demands philosophical sophistication about metaphysical grounding relations. Truthmaker epistemology appears committed to a problematic foundationalism where basic knowledge must be of truthmaker-involving facts, yet our actual epistemic practices suggest that knowledge often proceeds without any awareness of truthmakers. Williamson's charge that truthmaker theorists illegitimately project epistemic concerns onto metaphysics gains force from the Presentation Problem: we typically think about objects and properties directly rather than about truthmaking relations, suggesting that truthmakers play no essential role in actual belief formation or justification. However, truthmaker theorists can respond that the theory concerns the metaphysics of truth rather than the psychology of belief, and that epistemic access to evidence that indicates truthmakers suffices for knowledge without requiring direct truthmaker acquaintance. This defense aligns truthmaker commitments with epistemic externalism, where justification depends on objective relations to truthmakers rather than internal access to these relations, though internalists will resist this consequence as showing that truthmaker theory builds controversial epistemological commitments into metaphysics.

Moral truthmaking faces the distinctive challenge that moral properties appear to supervene on natural properties without being reducible to them: any natural duplicate of our world is a moral duplicate, yet moral truths seem to outrun what natural facts alone can necessitate. The Supervenience Constraint requires that moral differences must trace to natural differences, but multiple realizability suggests that diverse natural configurations can ground the same moral facts, creating a Granularity Mismatch where moral truths operate at a coarser grain than their putative natural truthmakers. Expressivist quasi-realism attempts to appropriate truthmaker talk while denying genuine moral ontology, treating moral truths as projections that inherit truthmaker conditions from the natural facts they track without requiring sui generis moral truthmakers. However, this strategy faces the objection that genuine truthmaking requires robust realism: if moral claims lack real truthmakers, then either they are not genuinely true or truthmaker theory must acknowledge systematic gaps. The Open Question Argument might seem to show that moral truths lack natural truthmakers since competent speakers can always meaningfully question whether natural configurations ground moral facts, but this epistemological observation need not undermine metaphysical truthmaking relations that operate independently of our recognitional capacities. Ultimately, truthmaker theory appears to favor correspondence over deflationary approaches to truth by requiring that true propositions answer to reality through substantive grounding relations, though deflationists can respond that truthmaking talk merely elaborates the trivial schema that p is true iff p without adding metaphysical substance. The moral case thus crystallizes truthmaker theory's central tension: its ambition to provide systematic metaphysical explanations of truth requires either inflating ontology with controversial entities or accepting explanatory gaps that undermine its theoretical aspirations.
